You're going to be in trouble

It's the second week of September and I'm a teacher trying to deal with a child, Marlon, who's always getting into trouble. I have been given a list of standards, by my school, that my classroom environment must meet and Marlon is not meeting those standards. I've been given a detailed policy for dealing with his behaviour, but no matter how consistently I follow these guidelines Marlon is still disrupting everything. Now the headteacher walks into my classroom, takes a seat at the back and starts to watch. He silently observes as Marlon gets out of his seat, without asking permission, grabs Janine's pencil out of her hand and then fiercely argues with her that he didn't snatch it.

As school policy dictates I ask Marlon to give back Janine's pencil, and send Marlon on his way to fill in a reflection form in another class, which he takes begrudgingly, after a few tears. After the lesson I receive the following message from my phase leader: "Your classroom does not meet the standards that the school requires so you will not be able to teach your class tomorrow." I followed the policy but because my classroom and more specifically, Marlon, didn't meet the school's standards, I'm in trouble.

In this scenario, one which has played out in many schools with different degrees of incident and punishment for the teacher and the child, despite following protocol I have been punished. Policies are written to ensure standards can be met. They are supposed to act as the map to get from where you are currently to where you want to be. But I followed the policy; the standards were not met because other human beings, especially children, are unpredictable and don't always do as they are told. But I did. I did exactly as I was told, yet I am in trouble. Whatever your stance on which pedagogical approach best enables children to learn, it is universally accepted that in order to learn, mistakes must be made. In this model of management, leaders actively punish mistakes and create systems to stop them happening in the first place.

I was a teacher and leader at a school that was facing similar challenges to those of many schools across the country. There are a range of ways to improve the educational outcomes for our children. The challenges and the potential solutions are well known, verbosely written about and fiercely debated. I'm not going to try to convince anyone that our schools face a one-solution-fits-all kind of problem but I can say that there is one consistent and pervasive stumbling block to improving our education system. This trend in school leadership is pervasive and fundamentally flawed and means whatever actions you take, as the children say: "You're going to be in trouble."

I'm going to call this model of leadership: punitive leadership. Now that may sound extreme to those of you that have not worked under it. However, I know that most if not all teachers will be able to give you an accurate and scathing assessment of punitive leadership: it really is deserving of that name. There is one condition for leadership to qualify for this unwanted title: managers hold colleagues to account by threatening punishment if their employees do not do as they are told. This is so toxic in our schools because its poisonous influence leaches all the way down to the children.

It is now a few weeks after the lesson where Marlon snatched the pencil. I have resigned as assistant headteacher and will be leaving the school in eight days. Again, the headteacher enters my classroom, without acknowledging me or the children. The children are excitedly building 3D shapes using plans and elevations and there's a lot of chatter and movement. Both of these things go down like a lead balloon with my headteacher and worst of all the children can tell. After the headteacher leaves, we pack up the 3D building activity and open our maths books. Caspar puts his hand up and says "Did we have to stop because Mr Landrus doesn't like it when we talk?" Before I can respond, Kaydrian turns to Caspar abruptly: "Just write the date. Miss is already in enough trouble."

This interaction was the most stark and shocking example of how children are held back by the leadership and management of schools. The worst part of this whole incident is that Kaydrian and Caspar are ten years old and they didn't just sympathise with me, they empathised with me. My class of ten-year-olds believed, rightly, that I was in trouble, because they were acutely aware of what happened at their school when you weren't seen to be doing the right thing. They'd all received detentions and stints in the LSU for a whole host of infringements of policy, from dancing in the line to wearing the wrong colour socks. They'd been given the same punishment for far more serious wrongdoings too, such as physically or verbally hurting other children. If you receive the same feedback for hurting another person and talking when you've been asked not to, as an adult you'd question the fairness of the system. As a child a much more damaging trend of behaviour emerges.

On a blustery Tuesday afternoon, I was dismissing a year 4 class when three children ran past another teacher on their way to my dismissal line. One, two, maybe all three of them bumped into the teacher and hurt her. She called them back and told them that she'd been injured when they ran past her. They all stared at her blankly and then looked at me, ostensibly to step in and fix it. I asked all three what they needed to do to make it right and after a couple of seconds, Jessica said "You should give one of us a reflection sheet for physical contact." The two boys immediately chipped in and tried to exculpate themselves: "Miss it was an accident... I didn't mean to, I mean, I didn't actually do it. I think it was Jessica."

In the midst of the unfolding argument between the three, I interjected: "What about apologising to Miss Jenson? Would that help?" The silence that followed these questions was significant. It seemed as if this was the first time that it had occurred to any of them that taking responsibility for their behaviour and adapting it might be both the right thing to do and the thing that was going to prevent a punishment. And that's because that wasn't what past experience had taught them. Past experience had taught them that it was best to avoid responsibility, and as a consequence punishment, at all costs.

As a teacher and a leader in a school that operates according to this system I felt unable to fulfil any of my duties. Mistakes are a teacher's bread and butter, without them I cannot do my job. If you don't believe me, think about the last time you learnt a new skill, not just a new piece of information. Did you have one attempt, do it perfectly the first time and then move on with the rest of your day? No, because usually we have to do something a few times before we make the appropriate adjustments to do it better: we learn from our mistakes. Sometimes we need a helping hand to get better at a skill, someone more advanced in that skill to show us how to improve: a teacher. Even if we are naturally brilliant at something, generally we enjoy it and practice the new skill just for the pure joy it brings us.

If learning is a process reliant on constantly adapting to feedback, then why has this prohibitive style of leadership shackled professionals in the place where learning is supposed to be the primary focus? This is a question I have wrestled with ever since I stepped into a classroom and I believe it is because of the educational blame game. Who's taking responsibility when Tayvian doesn't get the GCSE results he needs to get into college? Is it Tayvian's fault? Is it his parent's fault? Is it his teacher's fault? Is it his head teacher's fault? Is it the fault of the education system? Where should the blame lie and does it have to be apportioned at all? We should be teaching children to take appropriate responsibility for their actions so they can learn to adapt their behaviour. As adults working in schools it is vital that we model how to do this.

Similarly, we need to show children how and when to accept praise. When Jolene reaches a reading age three years above her chronological age, all of her teachers lavish her with praise but none of the teachers or leaders acknowledge publicly or privately the hard work that has been put in reading with Jolene every day. The accountability spiral works far more often to blame and accuse than it does to praise and reward. Leaders fall into this cycle of blaming their colleagues as a mechanism for protecting their own interests. It's so much easier to judge and punish the fact that no learning is happening in that maths lesson in 4R than it is to intervene and show the teacher how learning can happen in a 4R maths lesson.

However, at my previous school this is what happened when the head teacher walked into 4R and saw that a teacher was struggling and the children were lost. The head teacher asked if the teacher would allow her to speak to the class and with permission she brought the whole class back together and modelled how to find remainders as decimals. She demonstrated what questions you could ask, the arithmetic strategies for division, the praise you might give, the pace to move the children on at and then she allowed the children to have another go at the activity that they had previously been struggling with. The head teacher then remained in 4R to team teach for the rest of the lesson, assisting with verbal and written feedback, answering questions and addressing misconceptions. Finally, when the children had been dismissed the head teacher asked how she could have enabled the children to make even more progress.

It is an inevitable part of life that things won't always go the way we'd like them to and people won't always live up to our expectations of them. It is the professional response to these disappointments and failings that is a true litmus test of an organisation. If your organisation is refusing to accept that mistakes will happen or that people will fall short it is unlikely to be using errors as tools of learning. So, the final step we all need to take is to assess the culture of our schools and organisations. If that is something you feel unable to do, at whatever level of experience you find yourself, you're probably going to be in trouble.

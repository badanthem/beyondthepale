You'll work it out

A year ago I wrote an article about my journey from teaching to product management. When I wrote this, I was 6 months into a new career and I was really struggling. I was trying to do all the things that I thought product managers should do. But grooming the backlog,* running sprint planning and conducting user interviews, was not cutting it. Dissatisfied with my performance and frustrated at my lack of development, I felt like I was failing on all fronts.

When I was teaching, I was sometimes a bit of a mess. In my 6 years in the classroom, I considered leaving weekly. I've never done a job that required so much of me. But despite doubting my capacity to do the job as well as the children deserved, I never once doubted the importance of my role. When I became a product manager I doubted the importance of my work every day. On the one hand, I didn't know how to be a product manager and my inexperience slowed my team down. On the other, I was exceptionally grateful to have an opportunity to try a new career. For a while, I decided the best course of action was to pretend everything was under control.

After a period of blissful ignorance, I started to get the impression I wasn't performing very well. I couldn't work out where I fitted within the business and was unclear about my responsibilities. Over time my doubts morphed into a constant nagging feeling that I was doing a terrible job. This is how I felt when I wrote:

I am so appreciative of my teammates who took a chance on a complete novice. It is one thing to want to develop your colleagues and another to shoulder the risk and cost it takes to develop someone with absolutely no experience.

Do the right thing

Last year I saw myself as a 'risk and a cost'. I tried to challenge this feeling of inadequacy by seeking validation that I was doing the right things. This probably would have helped had I not been working in a start-up as a product manager. In start-ups, 'the right thing to do' does not exist. There's a long list of mistakes you could make and each of these has an associated cost. Your job is to make the least bad decision and adapt as quickly as possible when you realise a better course of action exists.

Needless to say, the validation I sought was not forthcoming. The feedback I received fluctuated between negative and positive for months. I went looking for role models that were successful in the company. The development and the sales team had some of the most high performing people so I started copying them. Unfortunately I was not a developer or a salesperson. As I modelled myself on my colleagues I slid further away from developing as a product manager.

After a while, I stopped seeking feedback on my work. I thought that the solution to my problem was to continue to battle through on my own. I did not want to risk disrupting my team by leaning on them and owning up to my cluelessness. As a PM I should reduce the stress on the development team, not exacerbate it by moaning about my feelings of inadequacy. I may have been unclear about what I was supposed to be doing but I was crystal clear about what not to do. Unsettling the team was firmly in the 'what not to do' column.

But, after finishing work at midnight the week before Christmas, I stopped pretending everything was fine. If my work wasn't valuable I needed to change what I was doing. I told the team I didn't know if I was doing my job properly. I told my friends that I wasn't sure what was expected of me. I told my manager that I felt devalued and on their advice I began applying for other jobs.

Something Good

And so, in February, I was asked to ramp up my attempts to get a new position.

A year ago I probably would have complained about how I was treated. But, by blaming others I'd also miss the opportunity to reflect on my own behaviour. Over the past year, I have come to realise that my feelings of inadequacy are felt by so many others in every field. Imposter syndrome is much more common than we'd like it to be and it is a surprisingly difficult perspective to shift. My experience caused me to become fixated on other people's perception of my value. The moment I was challenged I sought a blueprint of how to be better. When there was no product manager to model myself on at the company, I gave up.

Usually, I'm quite resilient. However, during this episode I began to see feedback as a threat rather than a means to improve. To be a resilient learner you have to be prepared to make mistakes. In this role, I became so scared of making mistakes that I stifled my ability to learn. Despite the challenges I faced in my previous role, I value the lessons I learnt in my time there. Personally, I needed a change and I have now found an environment that I can thrive in.

Starting a new career was like starting from scratch all over again. This gave me a new perspective on career development, particularly the role leaders play in growing the people they work with. As a PM I looked to others for validation, support and criticism far more than they looked to get those things from me. The encouragement to focus on improving my own practice was supposed to accelerate my development, but it had the opposite effect. When I was reading about how to be a PM and trying new things on my own, the anxiety about whether I was performing grew.

Great teachers get the best out of their students by understanding their needs and meeting them. Great leaders do the same. Leaders in business don't often have the benefit of spending hours getting to know their people. So how can leaders learn to support and develop people if they don't have time to build a relationship with them? The short answer is they can't. Leaders need to know the people they work with. If leaders take the time to get to know people, to listen to them, to find out what challenges they face then they can adapt the support they offer to meet the needs of the individual.

There is no secret formula for successful leadership. Even the most effective leaders fail and disappoint, if they didn't they wouldn't be human. How they take responsibility, regain trust and rebuild relationships when things go wrong acts as a model for how their colleagues respond to setbacks. Infallible leaders ensure that those around them hide failure rather than learn from it. Ultimately, leaders cannot afford to have unproductive, undeveloped and unhappy employees. Leaders must galvanise those around them to perform to the best of their abilities, the success of the business depends on it.

* Grooming the backlog: reviewing and prioritising the list of features and fixes waiting to be built.

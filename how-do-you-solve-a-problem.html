How do you solve a problem like me?

On a Wednesday evening in October I walked out of my job as an Assistant Headteacher in a primary school in East London. I had been a teacher since leaving university five years before and on that Wednesday evening, without a clue about what I was going to do next, I quit. The story of my sudden departure from teaching is a good one; full of toxic leadership, a flourishing blame culture and fundamentally incompatible views on the purpose of education. However, this journey began at the end of that story so just picture a teacher handing their lanyard to the headteacher and striding out the door without looking back. I wish that was how I had left. Instead, I crept into the head's office dropped a letter on his desk and ran out the building clutching my work boots, hoping no one would see me.

Flinging myself into unemployment for the first time in my life was terrifying. Every day, I spent hours scouring the internet for jobs I might be qualified to do outside of the classroom. The more I searched the more I found that my experience as a teacher and leader did not fit the bill for most job descriptions. My skill set was perfectly honed for another senior leadership position in a school, but I did not want to go back to school. Desperate for a dramatic change but clueless about what existed beyond the realm of education, outwardly I pretended to embrace the freedom of unemployment whilst internally I tried to shove down the rising tide of panic. Driven on by this combination of empowerment and fear, I set about looking for any job I was qualified to do, away from teaching, in the abyss of the internet.

Looking for a new job is challenging but searching for a new career is soul-destroyingly bleak. After a week I just wanted to read one job description that I could understand without KPI, ROI, B2B, UX, PMI, CRM or any other acronyms. Through a protracted process of trial and error, I found that searching for phrases like 'curriculum' and 'school improvement' brought up non-teaching jobs that I felt able to write applications for. After a month of this I got my first response! I happened to apply for a 'curriculum content specialist' job at Boclips and for the first time I felt like I might be semi-qualified for the job. I knew the curriculum like the back of my hand and although I wasn't entirely sure what a 'curriculum content specialist' did, I was encouraged before my initial call with Katy.

I heard about an educational video platform designed for use in the classroom and a company ready to tackle some significant problems for educators. The only slight bump in the road had been the role - despite my initial confidence Katy didn't seem to think 'curriculum content specialist' was the right position for me, instead she'd suggested that I apply for the 'product manager' role. After we'd had talked it through, and even though I had never heard of a 'product manager' I headed to the Boclips office, mostly just delighted that I had an interview at a real-life startup.

Since that initial call I have been crawling up, and sometimes sliding back down, a steep learning curve. I had two interviews in 10 days and spent the 9 days in between frantically researching product management. I have been extremely fortunate to find myself in a role that is so rich and varied; a role where I am encouraged to learn so much. If the luck of the role itself wasn't enough, I really hit the jackpot with the people I work with. I am so appreciative of my fellow Boclippers who took a chance on a complete novice. It is one thing to want to develop your colleagues and another to shoulder the risk and cost it takes to develop someone with absolutely no experience.

As for the transition from classroom to product management, it's been a surprising journey. I was not fully able to appreciate the best bits of teaching until I was out of the classroom. It was always full-on but the reward was immediate and tangible. Whatever my students were doing: having an idea, sharing a story, telling a joke, asking a question, playing superheroes in the playground and even having a tantrum, I was there to praise, pick them up and dust them off, help them tie their shoelaces or cut up their jacket potato. Bizarrely, product management offers some similar opportunities and rewards. I still do spend a fair amount of time praising, supporting and occasionally teaching, but I don't cut up any jacket potatoes.

However, I am still a problem solver. Granted, I no longer resolve some of the most pressing problems of our time such as who would win in a fight Thor or Voldemort? My role and my team demand me to deeply understand the problems of users. Much of what I read and hear about product management is about the logistics of landing on the 'best solutions', the solutions that will make the most money. For me the most critical part of my job is discovering and communicating the problems of our users, our stakeholders, our team and then facilitating the journey to viable, feasible and valuable solutions.

I hope that some of you work in hiring and talent management because without my head of talent I wouldn't know that the job I currently perform existed. First and foremost, some of the most brilliant people I have ever met are teachers and you are missing a huge pool of talent if you overlook them in your search for the right candidate. You may be thinking that you don't overlook teachers but they just never apply for your roles. There are two reasons for this: your job role requires specific technical skills that most teachers don't have or, and this is more likely, teachers are alienated by the job description; they may very well have the skills required to excel in the role but they don't believe they do.

Teachers read the classic 'you will be doing...' statement and immediately continue their search. How can they apply for a job where they will be playing a key role in 'adapting to and understanding the strategic process of our customers' when they've never worked with customers? If you want to tap into this pool of professionals take a leaf out of our hiring book and open up your job descriptions. Rather than introducing the role with what you'll be doing, open with an assurance. Ideally something along the lines of 'You don't need to know the <insert role here> playbook. We care more about who you are and your potential than the skills you have today. This means we will ramp you up on anything needed to succeed. But, prepare to learn fast.' Go on, test it out: I think you'll be pleasantly surprised at the change you see in the applications you receive.

Finally, to those of you that want and need a change from the classroom get searching and don't limit yourself. Apply to roles that seem well beyond the scope of your experience and qualifications. Be resilient and persistent, you will encounter far more companies that will be dismissive than receptive but get out there and prepare to learn fast!

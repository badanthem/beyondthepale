Drive with doubt

My startup is perfect. It has stellar people, plenty of runway, product market fit and an organisational culture of legend. Said nobody ever.

It benefits people peddling their 'how to grow a billion dollar company' wares to pretend there is a promised land of startup perfection where your company grows into a fully fledged unicorn in three to five years. If you have been fortunate enough to work in a startup that has been successful I'm sure you'd agree that very little about the early days were passable let alone perfect. Startups are hot messes. Every decision is like the paddles in a pinball machine, sending the whole organisation careening into walls and potentially sentencing the company to a final game over.

First things first, we all need to accept that a lot of things will go horribly wrong. You and everyone you work with will make many, many mistakes. These mistakes happen because we are navigating in unchartered waters, we don't know they're mistakes until we've made them. If you can frame success as a struggle against the state of chaos that all startups exist in then the inevitable and numerous challenges you face will build organisational resilience rather than threaten it.

This probably seems counterintuitive. To found or join a company at an early stage you must have a steely conviction that you've really got something here. You have to be willing to gamble that the sizeable investment of time and money will pay off in the long run. You also need a healthy dose of doubt and pragmatism to make the least bad decisions. Don't believe your own hype. The story you need to tell to raise money, get people to work with you and sell your pipe dream must coexist with a sense of realism about the probability of success.

Acknowledgement and acceptance of the fallibility of your ideas will not only keep you interrogating your decisions but it will transform the culture of your company for the better. The undying conviction that your company can be successful is your foot on the gas. Doubts keep your eyes on the road and your hands on the wheel. The road ahead is full of potholes and dead ends so expect a bumpy ride.

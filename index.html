What's the worst that could happen?

Why you should always check your work

The Problem

Every day, millions of people ask AI tools to help them think. Write these emails. Summarise these documents. Tell me what to do.

And the tools oblige: fluently, confidently, instantly.

The problem is that AI gets things wrong. When you hand the thinking to the machine, you stop maintaining the internal reference point that sets your spidey senses tingling when something looks off.

Researchers call this cognitive surrender. A calculator executes the operations you specify. AI decides which operations to perform. AI carries your bags and decides what to pack. It also changes the contents every time you ask it to pick them up.

The people most at risk of falling into this trap are those with just enough familiarity to feel confident, and not enough to know what they're missing.

What determines how much checking you need to do

Think about two journeys.

You land at an unfamiliar airport and navigate to somewhere you've never been. You are fully present. Checking the map constantly. Asking: does this look right? Is this the direction I expected?

Now do the same journey to your own home. The route becomes invisible. Automatic. You could do it in your sleep.

The familiar route is where you are most likely to make a mistake because you've outsourced the navigation to habit.

This is exactly how most people treat AI outputs. Familiar enough, probably fine, no need to verify. When actually, every AI output is the airport in a foreign city. You are in unfamiliar territory whether it feels that way or not.

Here is the underlying principle: how much of the work your prior knowledge can do for you determines how much checking you need to do consciously.

  • Zero prior knowledge: everything is signal. You have to attend to all of it.
  • Mastery: pattern recognition handles most of it automatically. This is efficient but it creates blind spots exactly where you think you need to check them least.
  • The dangerous zone: enough familiarity to feel confident. Not enough to actually be reliable.

Who this is for

Some people were handed tools for thinking through their education, the environment they grew up in. Those tools feel like common sense to the people who have them but they're learned. And because they are invisible, they are often ignored.

This framework is an attempt to name the thing that people with cultural capital do without noticing. A means for improving everyone's use of the digital tools and information available to them.

The Framework

First: The Dr Pepper Check

What's the worst that could happen?

Before you apply any checking method, ask: if this is wrong, what is the most terrible consequence of this being wrong?

This is not a formal risk assessment. It is a gut check that tells you how much verification the situation actually warrants. Low stakes: move on. High stakes: invest more time.

This is the only check that decides whether to check at all. Run it first, every time. If the worst that could happen is worth avoiding, invest in some of the following checks.

1. Inverse

Reverse the logic. Does it hold?

Work backwards from the conclusion. If the answer is right, reversing the logic should still make sense. If it produces a contradiction or an absurdity, the original answer deserves another look.

If 6 × 7 = 42, then 42 ÷ 7 should equal 6.

If the model recommends strategy A because of factor X, removing factor X should make strategy A less compelling. Does it?

2. Repeat

Do it again differently. Do you get the same result?

Re-run the question with different phrasing, a different approach, at a different time. Stable answers are more trustworthy than answers that shift with the wind.

A result that only appears once, under specific conditions, is a fluke until proven otherwise.

3. Estimate

Does this look right against what you already know?

Apply your own prior knowledge as a sense check. Not just "does this feel right" but "does this cohere with what I know to be true from experience, data, or direct observation?"

This is the check most commonly outsourced to the tool itself. Maintaining an independent reference point is vital for evaluating any output.

4. Triangulate

Does a completely different route arrive at the same place?

Use a different method, not just different phrasing of the same question. Solve the problem a different way. Consult a different source or apply a different framework entirely.

Triangulation checks whether the answer holds across fundamentally different approaches.

5. Boundary Test

Does it hold at the extremes?

What happens when you push the conditions to their limits? Apply the answer to an edge case. What if the scale is ten times larger? What if the context changes significantly? What if the assumptions are reversed?

Answers that are locally correct but don't generalise will fail here.

6. Peer Check

What does another person think?

Have someone else verify independently - not someone who will confirm what you already believe, but someone who will engage honestly with the problem. Explain your reasoning. Their questions will show you where the gaps are.

The goal is to get an independent perspective and to combat confirmation bias.

7. Adversarial Check

Try to break it.

Assume the answer is wrong. Argue the opposite. Ask: what would someone need to believe to think this conclusion is completely wrong?

Most verification is collaborative. Adversarial checking assumes the conclusion has not earned its credibility yet.

8. Longitudinal Check

Does it hold over time?

Some things look right immediately but their validity degrades as time goes on. Some conclusions were situationally correct but not durable. Build in a moment to return to the decision and ask: does this still hold, now that time has passed and conditions have changed?

This is a slow method. It catches the most expensive mistakes.

Adjusting the aperture

Not every check applies to every situation. The skill is knowing which ones to apply and at what depth.

Ask yourself:

  • How familiar is this territory? The less you know, the more consciously you need to check.
  • What did the Dr Pepper check tell you? Higher stakes warrant more methods, applied more rigorously.
  • Can you undo this if it's wrong? Irreversible decisions warrant more checking than ones you can course-correct later.
  • Can you identify where this came from? Untraceable outputs deserve more scrutiny.

Match the depth of checking to the depth of the problem. This framework is designed to keep you in the driver's seat and out of the trap of using any tool in a way that removes you from your own decision-making.

What's the worst that could happen?

Why you should always check your work

The Problem

Every day, millions of people ask AI tools to help them think. How should I respond to this message? What does this information tell us about our customers? Can you just do it for me?

And the tools do what you ask.

The problem is that AI isn't thinking. So when you stop maintaining the internal reference point that sets your spidey senses tingling when something looks off, you are likely to make some pretty significant mistakes.

Researchers at Wharton coined this position 'cognitive surrender'. Remember those magic 8 balls filled with blue coloured alcohol that would answer any question you asked with helpful responses like 'ask again later'? AI is like one of those but with infinite, often credible-sounding confidently presented answers. Would you surrender your decision making to a Magic 8 Ball?

What determines how much checking you need to do

Think about two journeys.

You land at an unfamiliar airport and need to get a train to somewhere you've never been. You are fully present throughout the journey. You check the map constantly and pay attention to every sign, stop and announcement. You might even ask someone else to confirm you are going the right way.

Now do the same journey from your home to work. Most days you probably can't even recall the steps you took to get to or from either place. You were on auto pilot, going through the motions without even thinking about them.

The familiar route is where you are most likely to make a mistake because over time we all outsource the navigation to habit.

Unfortunately this is how we're starting to treat AI outputs. They're familiar, almost certainly good enough. But every AI output is journey we've never done before, in a place where we don't speak the language. We are all in unfamiliar territory whether it feels that way or not.

Here is the underlying principle of this approach: there is only upside to checking you're going in the right direction.

  • Zero prior knowledge: Everything is new and potentially a signal that you need to pay attention to. You have to attend to all of it.
  • Mastery: pattern recognition handles most of it automatically. This is efficient but it creates blind spots exactly where you think you need to check them least.
  • Seen it before: enough familiarity to feel confident. Not enough to actually be reliable.

Who this is for

Some people are lucky enough to have been given a head start developing some of the tools that improve decision making. Those tools feel like common sense to the people who have them but they are learned.

This framework is an attempt to name the thing that people with the appropriate cultural capital do without noticing. A means for improving everyone's use of the digital tools and information available to them.

The Framework

First: The Dr Pepper Check

What's the worst that could happen?

Before you apply any checking method, ask this: if this is wrong, what is the most terrible thing that could happen?

This is a gut check that tells you how much verification the situation actually warrants. If the worst that can happen is trivial move on, if it's significant invest more time checking.

This is the only check that decides whether to check at all. Ask it first and ask it often.

1. Inverse

Reverse the logic. Does the conclusion still hold true?

If the answer is right then when you reverse the logic it should still make sense. If it produces a contradiction or an absurdity then the original answer deserves another look.

If 6 × 7 = 42, then 42 ÷ 7 should equal 6.

If the model recommends strategy A because of factor X, removing factor X should make strategy A less compelling. Does it?

2. Repeat

Do it again differently. Do you get the same result?

Re-run the question with different phrasing, a different approach, at a different time. Stable answers are more trustworthy than answers that shift dramatically.

A result that only appears once, under specific conditions is far more likely to be a fluke..

3. Estimate

Does this look right against what you already know?

Apply your own prior knowledge as a sense check. Ask "does this cohere with what I know to be true from experience, data, or direct observation?"

This is the check most commonly outsourced to the tool itself but would you allow a Magic 8 Ball to make that call for you?

4. Triangulate

Does a completely different route take you to the same answer?

Use a different method, try solving the problem in a different way. Consult a different source or apply a different framework entirely.

Triangulation checks whether the answer holds across fundamentally different approaches.

5. Boundary Test

Does it hold at the extremes?

What happens when you push the conditions to their limits? Apply the answer to an edge case. What if the scale is ten times larger? What if the context changes significantly?

Answers that are locally correct but don't generalise will fail here.

6. Peer Check

What does another person think?

Have someone else verify independently - not someone who will confirm what you already believe, but someone who will engage honestly with the problem. Their questions will hopefully illuminate some of your blindspots.

The goal is to get an independent perspective and to combat confirmation bias.

7. Adversarial Check

Try to break it.

Assume the answer is wrong and argue the opposite. Ask: what would someone need to believe to think this conclusion is completely wrong?

Most verification is collaborative. Adversarial checking assumes the conclusion has not earned its credibility yet.

8. Longitudinal Check

Does it hold over time?

Some things look right immediately but their validity degrades as time goes on. Some conclusions were situationally correct but not durable. Build in a moment to return to the decision and ask: does this still hold, now that time has passed and conditions have changed?

This is a slow method. It catches the most expensive mistakes.

Adjusting the aperture

Not every check applies to every situation. The skill is knowing which ones to apply and at what depth.

Ask yourself:

  • How familiar is this territory? The less you know, the more consciously you need to check.
  • What did the Dr Pepper check tell you? Higher stakes warrant more methods, applied more rigorously.
  • Can you undo this if it's wrong? Irreversible decisions deserve more time checking than ones you can course-correct later.
  • Can you identify where this came from? If you cannot understand how the position was arrived at then it might be best to ignore it.

This framework relies on you matching the depth of checking to the depth of the problem - this is subjective and can only be decided by you not by a Magic 8 Ball.
